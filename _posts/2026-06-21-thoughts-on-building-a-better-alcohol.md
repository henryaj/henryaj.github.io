</figure><p>Alcohol is a pretty nasty drug. </p><p>It causes cancer and birth defects. It has a very small <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Therapeutic_index">therapeutic index</a>, in that the ratio between an &#8216;effective dose&#8217; and a lethal dose is very small, much smaller than e.g. benzodiazepines or most pharmaceuticals. The more you drink, the higher your risk of heart disease, stroke, liver disease, and a variety of cancers. It combines synergistically, in rather bad ways, with a bunch of other things &#8211;&nbsp;in combination with cocaine if forms the highly-cardiotoxic <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cocaethylene">cocaethylene</a>, in combination with cigarettes it increases your risk of throat cancer. It reduces your insulin sensitivity.</p><p>It screws with your sleep even in small doses. A friend of a friend got a WHOOP band and said &#8220;it was a very expensive way of telling me I should stop drinking.&#8221;</p><p>Alcohol is really just a solvent &#8211; which means it gets everywhere, causing mayhem as it does. It interacts with a bunch of different receptors in ways which aren&#8217;t totally desirable. GABA to make you relaxed, glutamate to make you dissociated (and stumble around at higher doses); it triggers dopamine and opioid release to make you want more of it. One particular opioid receptor polymorphism makes it bind to opioid receptors directly; unsurprisingly people who have this mutation are much more likely to be alcoholics.</p><p>Folks used to say that a small amount is good for you (usually framed as a small glass of red wine per day, for the polyphenol content). The WHO now says that this isn&#8217;t true and that the only safe dose is zero.</p><p>Hangovers alone are a huge source of economic loss (in terms of workdays lost to being hungover) and raw suffering.</p><p>If that all wasn&#8217;t bad enough, it&#8217;s also full of calories.</p><p>It&#8217;s also <em>quite a lot of fun</em>, and it&#8217;s hugely significant culturally for a bunch of reasons. It&#8217;s an important part of meeting a mate, although a fraught one &#8211;&nbsp;most date rapes are committed with the help of alcohol alone; surprisingly few involve rohypnol or other drugs. It&#8217;s part of our social schemata for making new friends and bonding with old ones, getting to know your colleagues better. It&#8217;s a nice way to unwind after a long day at work, or a stressful encounter (&#8220;I need a stiff drink!&#8221;), or just to kick back on a lazy Sunday.</p><p>Any analysis of alcohol needs to consider the benefits as well as the costs &#8211; some think that the world would be robustly better without alcohol but I&#8217;m not so sure. Supposedly a lot of the issues that Gen Z face (fewer friendships and romantic relationships, more sexlessness) are in part to do with reduced risk-taking; it wouldn&#8217;t surprise me if that generation&#8217;s near-teetotalism was part of the problem. How many relationships were started with the aid of a little inebriation?</p><div><hr></div><p>Making an alcohol-like drug which isn&#8217;t quite so terrible would be an enormous improvement. It&#8217;s one of the most widely-used recreational drugs worldwide. Reducing the damage it does even a few percentage points would be a welfare increase on the order of tens of billions of dollars.</p><p>I see a few routes to doing this, some realistic and others not:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Combine a bunch of other, safer drugs which mimic the receptor binding profile of alcohol.</strong> Benzodiazepines are GABAergic, ketamine antagonises NMDA receptors, etc. You presumably could mimic the effects of alcohol pretty closely this way, although some of the downsides would remain.</p></li><li><p><strong>Do the above but without trying to mimic the full effects of alcohol.</strong> Your new alcohol would ideally not be opioidergic, for example; arguably you wouldn&#8217;t want it to be an NMDA antagonist either given that&#8217;s responsible for the loss of motor control and all the drunks who&#8217;ve fallen over showing up in casualty.</p></li><li><p><strong>Develop a completely new drug which mimics the effects of alcohol</strong>, without the side effects and with a readily-available antidote.</p></li></ul><p>In the next instalment, I&#8217;ll dig into each of these.</p>
