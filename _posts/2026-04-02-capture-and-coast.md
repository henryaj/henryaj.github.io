<p>Have a look at two versions of the same post, one on <a href="https://blog.sus.cat/p/dying-with-whimsy">Substack</a> and one on <a href="https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/3uRGPDrucg9RLLcp5/dying-with-whimsy">LessWrong</a>.</p><p>With even a cursory glance, it seems clear that the LessWrong interface is much better than Substack&#8217;s. Footnotes appear inline as Tufte-style sidenotes, meaning they can be seen alongside their context without having to scroll to the end of the page. (In a print book, having footnotes at the end of the page or endnotes at the end of the chapter makes sense &#8211; they&#8217;re a space-constrained medium &#8211; but on the web there&#8217;s no reason for this.)</p><figure>
  <img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!cOwh!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe0093749-99ae-4224-88ac-5948041201f9_2288x814.png" alt="" loading="lazy">
  <figcaption>Sidenote on LessWrong</figcaption>
</figure><p>Comments on LessWrong are much, <em>much</em> better than on Substack. (The whitepill here is that it&#8217;s possible for a small, mission-driven team to produce better software than a billion-dollar business.) <a href="https://wordpress.org/documentation/wordpress-version/version-2-7/">WordPress has had nested comments since 2009</a> and Substack&#8217;s implementation is <em>still worse somehow</em>.</p><p>There&#8217;s very little visual hierarchy, making the boundaries between comments hard to scan. Comments are strictly plain text, with no ability to format them or tag other commenters. Until recently you had to click through from an article page to see the comments section, and to view comments nested more than one layer deep you need to click through again. [EDIT: you actually still need to do this, but not on Astral Codex Ten, weirdly, which makes me think there&#8217;s some special mode for larger Substacks.] You can &#8216;like&#8217; comments but you can&#8217;t give other reactions, you can&#8217;t downvote, you can&#8217;t tag other people, you can only sort by date, collapsing threads is done by clicking in an unintuitive place.</p><p>It&#8217;s dogshit.</p><p>(Substack has a bunch of other ridiculous limitations, like no support for <a href="https://giphy.com/gifs/netflix-i-think-you-should-leave-tim-robinson-itysl-PyZoZpCSM4dDyqU40D">tables</a>.)</p><h2>Platform ossification</h2><p>Why am I bitching about comments so much? Because this sort of feature downgrade is endemic anywhere there&#8217;s platform lock-in. The product simply <em>doesn&#8217;t need to be as good</em> &#8211; the network is the product, and if you can&#8217;t get eyeballs on your blog unless it&#8217;s on Substack then Substack doesn&#8217;t need to have anything like feature parity with other platforms.</p><p>Platforms innovate, then once they have the network, they stop improving.</p><p>The same is true of the social media platform formerly known as Twitter. At its inception it was something genuinely new; at-handles, hashtags and retweets were all genuinely innovative. Then they spent a decade copying other platforms&#8217; features (mostly badly), and latterly Musk fired 80% of the engineering staff, which left the platform functional but ossified. Longstanding bugs never get fixed. The network lock-in is now so strong there&#8217;s no reason to &#8211; it&#8217;s the internet&#8217;s <em>de facto</em> public space. Good luck getting your shitty Mastodon &#8220;toots&#8221; to have even a hundredth of the reach of your tweets.</p><p>A number of other examples of this kind of ossification/enshittification come to mind:</p><ul><li><p>Slack search is still horribly broken</p></li><li><p>Reddit is actively hostile to its users &#8211; lots of gating that requires you either to sign in or download the mobile app, plus they killed popular third-party apps like <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_(app)">Apollo</a> overnight)</p></li><li><p>Google Maps&#8217; UI seems to get progressively worse and harder to use with every passing year</p></li><li><p>Instagram fought chronological feeds for years after switching to an algorithmic feed (there was even a #RIPInstagram campaign to get them to switch back)</p></li></ul><h2>But platforms do add new features!</h2><p>There <em>is</em> some amount of innovation going on &#8211; usually driven by a competitor implementing a new feature (think Stories being added literally everywhere, or Twitter Spaces as a response to <a href="https://www.clubhouse.com/">Clubhouse</a>). Instagram Reels and YouTube Shorts were a response to TikTok&#8217;s ascendency. (Maybe this post should have been called &#8220;the enTikTokification of everything&#8221;.)</p><p>And those features are horrible! I hate reels! I have to install a panoply of Chrome plugins to block Shorts! We ended up <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doomscrolling">inventing a word</a> to describe becoming horribly addicted to these things and collectively wasting billions of hours a day on them!</p><figure>
