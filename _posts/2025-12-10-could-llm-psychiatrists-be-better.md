<p><em>[Not medical advice, obviously]</em></p><p>I had a call with my psychiatrist &#8211; the first one in about a year; I hadn&#8217;t really felt the need to see him until then &#8211; and we spoke for a scant ten minutes. I gave a one-minute description of how I was feeling, and he fiddled with the dose of a mood stabiliser I take and that was it. </p><p>He didn&#8217;t attempt to really dig deeper into how I was feeling, give me a standard questionnaire like the <a href="https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/1725/phq9-patient-health-questionnaire9">PHQ-9</a> or <a href="https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/4058/montgomery-asberg-depression-rating-scale-madrs">MADRS</a> which would have given a numerical idea of how I was doing over time. The session cost &#163;150, which makes his hourly rate &#163;900. And he charged me &#163;15 for a private prescription which he forgot to issue.</p><p>Needless to say I didn&#8217;t really feel like he was taking me seriously.</p><p>(On the plus side, I&#8217;ve had medical professionals who were so disorganised they never even bothered to invoice me. How nice of them to give me a discount.)</p><p>I wondered if an LLM couldn&#8217;t do a better job than this guy.</p><p>So I went and downloaded a copy of the Maudsley<sup><a id="footnote-anchor-1" href="#footnote-1">1</a></sup> and shoved it into a Claude project. (It&#8217;s over 900 pages, so I trimmed the PDF to just the ~150 pages on depression first.) I gave it custom instructions to ensure it always checked and referenced the Maudsley guidelines.</p><figure>

</figure><p>Here is it dismantling some common myths (Opus 4.5):</p><p><em>User: how long does it take antidepressants to start working in depression?</em></p><blockquote><p>The Maudsley is clear on this, and it&#8217;s actually good news: <strong>the idea that antidepressants take 2&#8211;4 weeks to work is a myth</strong>.</p><p>All antidepressants show a response pattern where the rate of improvement is highest during weeks 1&#8211;2 and lowest during weeks 4&#8211;6. Statistical separation from placebo is seen at 2&#8211;4 weeks in single trials (which is where the &#8220;lag effect&#8221; idea comes from), but after only 1&#8211;2 weeks in meta-analyses. When large numbers of patients are treated and detailed rating scales are used, an antidepressant effect is statistically evident at 1 week.</p><p>In clinical practice using simpler observations, an antidepressant effect in an individual is usually seen by 2 weeks.</p><p>The practical implication: in those who will ultimately respond, virtually all will have begun to show at least minor improvement by 4 weeks. Those showing no discernible improvement at this point will very probably never respond to that drug at that dose. &#8230;</p></blockquote><p>Of course, with internet access it you can get state-of-the-art evidence, which is likely more up-to-date than what your psych knows.</p><p><em>User: what&#8217;s the evidence for pushing escitalopram to doses higher than 20mg? search online as well.</em></p><blockquote><p>The Maudsley says there&#8217;s <strong>minimal evidence for SSRI dose escalation in general</strong>, but specifically notes that <strong>escitalopram (along with venlafaxine and tricyclics) may be an exception</strong>.</p><p>The <strong>Furukawa 2019 meta-analysis</strong> found SSRIs plateau around 20&#8211;40mg fluoxetine equivalents (~10&#8211;20mg escitalopram). But a Bayesian reanalysis suggested escitalopram specifically can be escalated higher than other SSRIs&#8212;up to ~30mg for ages 30&#8211;65.</p><p>A <strong>Korean RCT (2019)</strong> found 30mg vs 20mg in non-remitters showed improved depressive symptoms on MADRS, but not increased response/remission rates&#8212;a modest continuous effect, not a categorical one.</p><p>An <strong>open-label pilot</strong> escalating to 50mg found median remission dose was 30mg, with 38% of remitters needing 50mg. Tolerability declined above 40mg.</p><p><strong>Why escitalopram might be different:</strong> It has a unique allosteric mechanism&#8212;binding to an allosteric site decreases its own dissociation rate from SERT, potentially potentiating effect at higher doses. Other SSRIs don&#8217;t do this.</p><p><strong>Safety:</strong> QTc increases ~10.7ms at 30mg&#8212;modest and generally not clinically concerning.</p><p><strong>Bottom line:</strong> 30mg is reasonable and better-supported than high doses of other SSRIs, though the evidence isn&#8217;t definitive.</p></blockquote><p>Certainly it seems like, if not a replacement for a traditional psychiatrist, an LLM + a copy of the Maudsley is very good foil for classical care.</p><div class="footnote"><strong>1.</strong> <p><a href="https://www.royaldevon.nhs.uk/knowledge-library-services/find-a-book-or-ebook/e-books/the-maudsley-prescribing-guidelines-in-psychiatry/">The Maudsley Prescribing Guidelines in Psychiatry</a> (<a href="https://archive.vn/kd8RH">a</a>):</p><blockquote><p>The Maudsley Prescribing Guidelines in Psychiatry is the essential evidence-based handbook on the safe and effective prescribing of psychotropic agents.</p><p>Covering both common and complex prescribing situations encountered in day-to-day clinical practice, this comprehensive resource provides expert guidance on drug choice, minimum and maximum doses, adverse effects, switching medications, prescribing for special patient groups, and more.</p><p>Each clear and concise chapter includes an up-to-date reference list providing easy access to the evidence on which the guidance is based.</p></blockquote><p></p></div>
